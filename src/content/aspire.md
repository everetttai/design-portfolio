# Aspire
### A learn-by-doing hub for the professional skills school doesn't teach

**Team:** 4 product designers (capstone team project for Dartmouth's Intro to UI/UX course) — I led the team through the project
**My role:** Project Lead, Research, UX, and Visual Design — directed the 4-person team through ideation, prototyping, and execution, while also contributing hands-on across research, UX, and visual design
**Timeline:** 3 weeks
**Deliverable:** Figma prototype

---

## The problem

Most career-prep resources tell you *what* to do — how to write a resume, how to answer a behavioral question — but never give you anywhere to actually practice doing it. Reading advice about interviews and being interviewed are not the same skill.

Our user interviews surfaced three consistent frustrations among college students trying to build professional skills on their own:

- **Wasting time finding the right resources.** Advice is scattered across blog posts, YouTube videos, and career-center PDFs, with no single place to go.
- **Lack of feedback while learning.** Reading about what makes a good elevator pitch doesn't tell you whether *your* elevator pitch is any good.
- **Lack of long-term motivation and structure.** Without a system tracking progress, practice fizzles out after a session or two.

That gap between passive learning and actual readiness shows up in the research on retention, too — passive methods like reading and watching videos top out around 5–30% retention, while active methods like practicing and teaching others reach 75–90%. Career prep is almost entirely taught the passive way.

We framed the opportunity as a single How Might We:

> How might we provide an easy way for people to learn and practice life skills?

---

## Research → principles

Synthesizing the interviews into a user journey map surfaced a clear emotional arc: people discover a new skill they want to build, feel motivated initially, then hit friction almost immediately — confusion about where to start, trial-and-error with no expert to ask, and eventually burnout from a lack of feedback or community. The opportunities clustered around the same points where sentiment dropped: faster feedback, a way to know if a tool is actually helping, and incentive to keep going when no one's watching.

![Our process diagram, research synthesis sticky notes, and user journey map](aspire/research_process)

That research converged into three design principles we held onto through the rest of the project:

1. **Convenient** — people waste time hunting for the right resource; the product should save them that time.
2. **Accessible** — the solution should work for all people, regardless of learning ability.
3. **Motivating** — learning involves a lot of failure, so the product needs to help people push through it rather than quit.

A few specific ideas from our brainstorming made it into the final product close to verbatim — including one of mine: a resume-help section that has users fill in blanks and choose action verbs from a word bank rather than starting from a blank page, which became the ACE-framework resume builder described below.

---

## From sketch to screen

We moved through paper sketches, to grayscale lo-fi wireframes, to a full hi-fi prototype inside the same three-week window — fast enough that we had to be deliberate about which flow actually needed to be polished. One of our own working notes from that phase put it plainly: *"Have one really good flow that can be your demo."* Rather than spreading effort evenly across every feature, we made sure the core practice loop — record a response, get feedback, try again — was the one that actually felt finished.

## The product

**Daily Questions** puts a fresh interview-style prompt in front of the student every day, recorded on video, with AI feedback generated immediately after — not a generic "good job," but specific notes like calling out a filler-word habit or pointing out an off-topic tangent. A start-over confirmation prevents an accidental re-record from losing a take the student was happy with.

**Exercises** organizes practice into named categories students actually asked for — Realtime Interview Prep, Writing Formal Emails, Résumé Building, CV Crafting, Elevator Pitches, and a "Random Practice" option for anyone who doesn't want to choose.

**Email Practice** gives a realistic scenario (a recruiter referral, a follow-up after an interview invite) and grades the drafted response against specifics — noting if a skill mentioned doesn't match the role, or if the email skips a relevant past experience that should be there.

**Résumé Building** walks students through the ACE framework (Action verb, Context, End result) one bullet at a time, rather than asking for a finished résumé up front — which directly reflects the word-bank idea from our brainstorm.

**My Library** centralizes every resume, cover letter, interview response, and document a student has created, organized into starred items, categories, and recents — so practice work doesn't disappear into the void after a session ends.

**Professionals** lets students request feedback from real industry professionals and book time with them directly, extending the AI feedback loop with the kind of human perspective our research said students still wanted.

**Learn** structures everything into chapters with visible completion progress, addressing the "long-term motivation and structure" problem directly rather than leaving practice unstructured.

## Reflection

For this project, I took on the role of project manager in addition to designing. I directed our team of four through ideation, prototyping, and execution — which meant the biggest things I learned weren't really about UI at all. I learned how to delegate tasks across a team, how to keep people motivated through a tight three-week sprint, and how to push the group toward deadlines without things stalling out. That's a different muscle than designing a screen, and it's one I hadn't really had to use before this.

This was also my first big project in Figma, and a lot of the learning was just mechanical: how to actually unify spacing, keep a consistent style across dozens of screens built by four different people, and make a file feel like one product instead of four people's individual work stitched together.

I'll say plainly that this was a starter project, and it looks it — some of it is rudimentary and incomplete. If I went back, I'd fix some of the sizing ratios and color choices that never got fully resolved in the three weeks we had. I'm including it in my portfolio anyway because the things I actually learned here — leading a team under a real deadline, and getting comfortable in Figma at a level I hadn't been before — mattered more to how I design now than the polish of any individual screen.
